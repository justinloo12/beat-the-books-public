Beat the Books — an open forecasting experiment

Can a hobby model out-forecast the betting market?

Almost certainly not. That is precisely why this is worth measuring properly.

Pre-registered 2026-07-04 · updated automatically twice daily · live report card

The question

This repository contains a fully automated MLB model: Statcast pitching and lineup data, park factors, weather, bullpens, a run simulation, and prices for every game on the slate. The interesting question is not whether it can pick winners — anything can pick winners half the time. It is whether its probabilities are better than the ones already embedded in the market's odds once the bookmaker's margin is removed.

Betting markets are brutally efficient at baseball. The honest prior is that the market wins this comparison. Most public models never confront that prior, because they only publish their bets — a handful of hand-picked games — and small samples can flatter anything for months.

The method

So this experiment scores the model on every game it prices, roughly fifteen a day, not just the five or so that clear the betting threshold. Each morning the pipeline logs, for every game: the model's home-win probability and the market's no-vig probability, both frozen at first publication. Each night a robot grades them against final scores and recomputes the running Brier score — mean squared error of probability against outcome — for the model and for the market on identical games.

  • The log is append-only; a prediction, once written, is never revised.
  • Grading and publication are automated. There is no step where a human decides whether the numbers look good enough to post.
  • The rules were frozen in advance, including what would count as success: model Brier below market Brier over at least 1,000 games, and positive closing-line value over at least 300 picks. Below 200 graded games the dashboard is required to call the comparison noise.

The verdict so far

Status at pre-registration, July 4, 2026

Over the 98 backfilled games with market odds, the model's Brier score is 0.2442 against the market's 0.2402. The market is ahead. On picks, 52 graded bets show no evidence of edge. Every current number sits far below the pre-declared noise floor, so the only defensible conclusion is: no evidence of skill, market ahead, sample too small — keep counting.

By October the log should hold 1,300+ graded games. If the model is genuinely worse than the market, this page will say so with real statistical weight behind it. If it somehow is not, the same numbers will say that instead. The point of pre-registration is that we do not get to choose.

What would change the verdict

  • Model Brier moving below market Brier and staying there as the sample passes 1,000 games.
  • Sustained positive CLV once 300+ picks are graded — the market moving toward the model's prices after they are published.
  • Calibration buckets converging on the diagonal: when the model says 58%, home teams winning about 58% of the time.

Anything short of that — a hot fortnight, a lucky streak of picks, a good-looking 30-day window — is noise, and the protocol forbids promoting it to a conclusion.

Follow along

The dashboard regenerates this experiment's report card on every refresh: the cumulative Brier race, the calibration table, and the 30-day rolling comparison, each labelled with its sample size. The protocol (machine-readable copy) and the full source and data are public. Nothing on this page is betting advice; it is a measurement exercise.
